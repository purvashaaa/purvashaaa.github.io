Research Paper · EC705 Economics of Health and Education

Impact of Family Health Shocks on Children's Educational Outcomes

Ordered Logit District Fixed Effects IHDS-II Data Causal Inference R N = 10,211

This paper examines how short-term household health shocks — measured as disability days due to illness — affect children's academic outcomes in India. Using IHDS-II (2011–12) data on 10,211 children aged 8–11, we find that each additional day of household health shock significantly reduces the log-odds of achieving higher writing levels. The results highlight the need for integrated health and education policy interventions.

Research Question & Motivation

When a family member falls ill in India, the consequences extend well beyond health. Children may be pulled out of school to provide caregiving, household income shrinks as working adults miss days, and psychological stress impairs learning even for children who remain enrolled. Yet most research has focused on chronic conditions like malnutrition — leaving short-term health shocks relatively understudied.

This paper bridges that gap by asking: does a temporary household health shock, measured as days lost to disability, causally reduce children's academic performance? Using data from the India Human Development Survey (IHDS-II, 2011–12), we analyse test scores in reading, writing, and mathematics for children aged 8–11 across India.

Data

The IHDS-II is a nationally representative survey conducted jointly by the University of Maryland and NCAER, covering 42,152 households across 1,503 villages and 971 urban neighbourhoods. Its key advantage over alternatives like NSS or NFHS is that it combines direct test-score assessments for children with household-level health data — allowing a direct link between parental illness and children's learning outcomes.

Our sample is restricted to 10,211 children aged 8–11 with available test scores and health shock information. Health shock is defined as days lost to disability (due to ailments such as diarrhoea, typhoid, malaria) for any household member excluding the child, in the past 30 days.

Methodology

Because educational outcomes (writing: 0–2, reading: 0–4, maths: 0–3) are ordinal rather than continuous, OLS would be inappropriate — it would treat the categories as cardinal. We instead use an Ordered Logit model, which correctly models the probability of being at or below each outcome category.

Results & Discussion

Writing level: HH_health_shock coefficient = −0.0098, significant at 5% (p = 0.027). Each additional day of household disability reduces the log-odds of achieving a higher writing level by ~0.98%.

Reading and maths: Coefficients are negative but not statistically significant, suggesting these subjects may be more reinforced at school, mitigating household disruption effects.

Marginal effects: An additional health shock day increases the probability of being at the lowest writing level by 0.136 percentage points and decreases the probability of the highest writing level by 0.141 percentage points (both significant at 5%).

Ability is the strongest predictor across all outcomes — an additional unit of innate ability increases the probability of higher writing, reading, and maths levels by 32.9%, 43.3%, and 45.7% respectively. Lower caste groups (OBC, Dalit, Adivasi) and Muslim households face significantly larger negative impacts, reflecting compounding socioeconomic disadvantage.

Village-level infrastructure variables (schools, health centres, roads) were largely insignificant once district fixed effects and individual controls were included — suggesting quality of schooling matters more than proximity.

Policy Implications

The findings underscore the need for integrated health and education policy. Schools need mechanisms to support children returning after household health disruptions — such as self-paced digital learning modules, one-on-one catch-up guidance, and reduced-cost health insurance to shield low-income families from the educational spillovers of illness.

Existing government schemes like Sarva Shiksha Abhiyan and the National Health Mission address these goals in parallel, but implementation gaps — particularly in rural areas — mean their protective effects remain incomplete.
