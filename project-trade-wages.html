Free Trade, Fair Outcomes? Trade Liberalisation & Industry-Level Wage Dynamics in Post-Reform India

Fixed Effects Panel Regression Structural Break (1991) Difference-in-Differences RBI KLEMS Data 27 Industries · 1980–2022 N = 1,161 R

Using 40 years of industry-level panel data from the RBI KLEMS database, this paper examines whether India's 1991 trade liberalisation produced differential wage outcomes across export-oriented and non-export industries. A fixed-effects regression with a structural break at 1991 finds that export-oriented industries earned a statistically significant real wage premium of approximately 21–24% post-liberalisation — consistent with New Trade Theory but raising persistent distributional concerns.

Solo Paper — Key Focus Areas

Independent research paper. Core contributions: construction of the wage variable from KLEMS labour share, gross output, and employment data; fixed-effects panel regression with structural break specification; parallel pre-trends validation; robustness checks with year fixed effects; interpretation of the interaction coefficient in percentage terms using log specification.

Data Construction

The RBI KLEMS database does not directly report industry wage rates. Real wages were constructed from available variables using the following formula:

Wage_it = (LS_it × GO_it) / L_it

where LS_it is the labour income share in gross output, GO_it is gross output in constant prices, and L_it is total employment for industry i at time t. This produces real wage per worker consistent across industries and over time, capturing the share of production attributable to labour divided by number of workers employed.

Industries were classified into two groups based on their historically dominant participation in India's merchandise export basket (sourced from RBI and DGCI&S data). Export-oriented industries include textiles, chemicals, basic metals, machinery, electrical and optical equipment, transport equipment, and other high-export-share manufacturing sub-sectors. Non-export industries include agriculture, food products, construction, electricity and water supply, trade, and services.

Empirical Specification

The main regression specification is a fixed-effects panel model with a structural break at 1991:

ln(Wage_it) = α + β₁Trend_t + β₂Post1991_t + β₃Export_i + β₄(Export_i × Post1991_t) + γᵢ + δt + ε_it

The coefficient of interest is β₄ — the interaction between export status and the post-1991 dummy. This captures the differential wage trajectory of export-oriented industries after liberalisation, net of any general post-1991 trend (captured by β₂) and any time-invariant industry-level wage advantage (captured by β₃ and γᵢ).

Pre-liberalisation parallel trends were formally validated: export and non-export industries followed statistically comparable wage paths prior to 1991, satisfying the key identifying assumption. The log-wage specification minimises heteroskedasticity and allows direct percentage interpretation of coefficients. Year fixed effects absorb global shocks including the 1997 Asian crisis, the 2008 financial crisis, and COVID-19.

Interaction term (β₄ = 0.2186): Export-oriented industries earned approximately 21.8% more in real wages post-1991 compared to non-export industries, holding industry and year fixed effects constant. Using the semi-elasticity formula (e^0.2186 − 1) × 100 ≈ 24.4% in levels.

Post1991 coefficient (−0.406): The negative baseline Post1991 dummy reflects short-run adjustment effects following liberalisation — a temporary wage compression across all industries before the export premium materialised.

Trend coefficient (0.0282): Positive and significant — confirms a secular long-run upward wage trend across industries, independent of liberalisation effects.

High R² (0.945): Industry and year fixed effects together explain most of the variation in log wages, validating the panel structure and reducing omitted variable concerns.

Interpretation & Theoretical Fit

The results partially support classical Stolper-Samuelson predictions — in a labour-abundant economy like India, trade liberalisation should raise returns to labour in export-intensive sectors. The significant post-export interaction confirms this for manufacturing sub-sectors. However, a pure Stolper-Samuelson reading is weakened by the absence of uniform wage gains across all labour-intensive industries.

The results align most closely with New Trade Theory: wage gains concentrated in industries that could scale up and integrate into global value chains, not spread uniformly across the labour force. This has direct fair trade implications — the benefits of liberalisation are industry-specific rather than economy-wide, implying persistent inter-sectoral wage divergence even as aggregate wages rise.

Robustness & Limitations

Year fixed effects absorb all time-varying global shocks. Log specification ensures percentage interpretability and reduces heteroskedasticity. Pre-trend validation confirms the parallel trends assumption holds before 1991. Results are robust across specifications with and without year fixed effects (R² moves from 0.941 to 0.945).

Key limitations: the export dummy is binary rather than time-varying, which may miss industries that shifted export orientation post-reform. The wage variable is constructed from labour share data and may carry measurement error. Within-industry heterogeneity (skilled vs. unskilled, formal vs. informal) is not captured. Potential endogeneity from simultaneous capital flows and productivity shocks is noted — instrumental variable or dynamic panel approaches could strengthen causal identification in future work.
