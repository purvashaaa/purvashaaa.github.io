Predicting Restaurant Ratings: Econometric & Machine Learning Models

IV / 2SLSGMMXGBoostRandom ForestLasso / RidgeYelp DatasetR

This project compares the predictive power of 13 statistical and machine learning models on the Yelp Open Dataset, with the goal of predicting restaurant star ratings from structured business features. Using 30-fold cross-validation, XGBoost achieved the best performance (RMSE: 0.67, R²: 0.35), significantly outperforming all linear methods and confirming that restaurant ratings are driven by non-linear, interactive relationships.

Research Objective

Restaurant ratings are shaped by a complex mix of geography, cuisine type, service features, and social proof signals like review counts. The core question is whether this relationship is fundamentally linear — as OLS assumes — or whether non-linear interactions (e.g., cuisine × location, popularity × amenities) are driving the pattern.

This project tests both families of models on the Yelp Open Dataset, which contains structured business attributes for restaurants across the US and Canada. The assignment deliberately avoids causal claims — this is a pure prediction exercise — but the model comparison itself reveals important insights about the structure of rating behaviour.

Data & Preprocessing

The raw Yelp dataset contained nested JSON structures (business.json). Preprocessing involved: filtering to restaurant-only observations; flattening nested attributes into binary/categorical dummy variables; one-hot encoding for cuisine types and city/state identifiers; imputing missing fields; and standardising continuous features for penalised regression models.

The target variable (Y) is the restaurant's star rating (1–5). Predictors (X) include review count, home delivery availability, alcohol service, pricing tier, cuisine category, location coordinates, and operating hours.

Models Compared

Ordinary Least Squares
Linear benchmark. Assumes additive, linear relationships. Prone to multicollinearity with high-dimensional feature sets.
Ridge Regression
Shrinks all coefficients toward zero to remedy multicollinearity. Retains all features but reduces their magnitude.
Lasso & Post-Lasso
Sets irrelevant coefficients to exactly zero, performing automatic feature selection. Post-Lasso re-runs OLS on selected variables.
Elastic Net & LAVA
Elastic Net combines Ridge and Lasso. LAVA is a hybrid shrinkage model balancing bias and flexibility.
Random Forest
Ensemble of decision trees — reduces variance by averaging across many trees. Captures non-linear relationships and interactions.
XGBoost
Gradient boosted trees — sequentially corrects errors of previous trees. State-of-the-art for tabular prediction tasks.
Neural Network (MLP)
Multi-layer perceptron — flexible non-linear learner. Strong in principle but showed marginal gains over XGBoost here.
Single Decision Tree
Simple interpretable rules. Worst performer due to high variance and tendency to overfit without ensemble averaging.

Key Insights

Tree-based ML models significantly outperform all linear methods. This confirms that the mapping from business characteristics to star ratings is not additive or linear — non-linear interactions between cuisine type, location, and service features are crucial.

All penalised regressions (Lasso, Ridge, Elastic Net) perform nearly identically to OLS, suggesting the feature relationships are dense rather than sparse — Lasso's variable selection gains are limited when most predictors matter.

Fast Food is the dominant feature in both Random Forest and XGBoost importance rankings — suggesting cuisine category drives rating behaviour more than any service or location feature.

R² below 0.35 for all models implies a large unobserved component — customer experience, service quality, and review text carry information that structured features alone cannot capture.

The natural extension of this work would incorporate NLP analysis of review text and temporal patterns in rating behaviour — both of which could substantially improve predictive accuracy.
