Consumer Demand & Welfare Analysis:
Tamil Nadu vs Maharashtra

Linear Expenditure System Almost Ideal Demand System Engel Curves Expenditure Elasticities NSSO 68th Round N ≈ 1,00,000 R

This paper applies the Linear Expenditure System (LES) and the Almost Ideal Demand System (AIDS) to NSSO 68th Round household data (~1 lakh observations) to compare consumption structures in Tamil Nadu and Maharashtra. By estimating subsistence floors, marginal budget shares, and expenditure elasticities, the study shows that Tamil Nadu's lower multidimensional poverty stems not from higher income but from a lower private subsistence burden — enabled by stronger public welfare infrastructure.

Research Question & Motivation

Maharashtra and Tamil Nadu have broadly comparable per capita incomes, yet Tamil Nadu's Multidimensional Poverty Index rate (2.20%) is dramatically lower than Maharashtra's (11.85%). Standard income-based frameworks cannot explain this divergence. This paper asks: do differences in household consumption structure — specifically subsistence requirements and supernumerary income allocation — explain the welfare gap?

The study draws on Stiglitz's argument that "economic growth is only a means to an end" and Drèze and Sen's observation that growth is a "leaky support" for human development. Maharashtra's GSDP of ₹45.3 lakh crore (13.5% of India's GDP) has not translated into proportionate welfare gains — pointing to structural inefficiencies in how income is converted into household welfare.

Data

The NSSO 68th Round Household Consumer Expenditure Survey (2011–12) covers approximately 1,01,662 households nationally, with sufficient state-level samples for robust demand system estimation. Expenditure is aggregated into four composite commodity groups: Food & Beverages, Clothing, Education & Medical (EduMed), and Miscellaneous.

Methodology

Linear Expenditure System (LES) — Stone-Geary

Maximises U = Σβᵢ ln(qᵢ − γᵢ) subject to budget constraint. The γᵢ parameters capture minimum required (subsistence) consumption; βᵢ captures how supernumerary income is allocated. Estimated as Engel curves — commodity expenditure regressed on total expenditure. Adding-up (Σβᵢ = 1) and regularity (X ≥ ΣPᵢγᵢ) conditions imposed. Yields own-price, cross-price, and expenditure elasticities.

Almost Ideal Demand System (AIDS) — Deaton & Muellbauer

Derived from PIGLOG preferences. Budget share equations wᵢ = αᵢ + Σγᵢⱼ log pⱼ + βᵢ log(x/P) estimated in Working-Leser form (price variation unavailable). Adding-up, homogeneity, and symmetry restrictions imposed. Negative βᵢ = necessity; positive βᵢ = luxury. State, social group, and religion fixed effects included. Goodness of fit: food R² = 0.41 (strong); EduMed R² = 0.11 (moderate).

Maharashtra's EduMed marginal share (0.41) is 66% higher than Tamil Nadu's (0.25) — meaning Maharashtra households must spend a far greater fraction of any income increase on private education and healthcare. This reflects weaker public provision, not higher welfare.

Tamil Nadu's higher Miscellaneous share (0.49 vs 0.39) indicates that once the lower subsistence burden is met, households have greater capacity for discretionary spending — a structural welfare advantage that income figures alone do not capture.

State-Level Welfare Interpretation

Maharashtra

  • Highest GSDP in India (₹45.3 lakh crore, 13.5% of national GDP)
  • Higher absolute household expenditure but unequal distribution
  • High EduMed marginal share — households bear private education/health costs
  • MPI poverty rate: 11.85% — growth has not translated to welfare
  • Greater expenditure variability signals within-state inequality

Tamil Nadu

  • GSDP ₹31.1 lakh crore, per capita income ₹3,15,220 (vs Maharashtra's ₹2,78,681)
  • Lower absolute spending but more equitable and stable distribution
  • Low EduMed marginal share — public welfare infrastructure absorbs costs
  • MPI poverty rate: 2.20% — welfare-first model demonstrably works
  • Higher discretionary income capacity reflects lower subsistence burden

Policy Implications

The central finding is that Tamil Nadu's welfare advantage is structural rather than income-driven. Strong public provision of education, healthcare, and food security reduces the private subsistence burden, allowing households to allocate income toward discretionary and welfare-enhancing consumption. Maharashtra's higher income growth has remained "leaky" — welfare gains have not reached the household level proportionately.

For high-poverty states, the priority should be reducing private subsistence costs: strengthening the PDS, expanding primary healthcare, and subsidising essential goods. Conditional cash transfers, scholarships, and skill development vouchers could further redirect income toward human capital categories where current elasticities are low. Critically, the results argue against uniform national welfare policy — state-specific consumption structures and public service availability require differentiated, regionally calibrated interventions.
